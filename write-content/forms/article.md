# Form: Article

Full treatment of a topic, crafted for a wider audience. A cold reader with
no prior context can follow it. Crafted for the audience, not just for the writer.

**Academic grounding:** Kinneavy's referential aim (reality-dominant).
Diátaxis: tutorial (exploratory), how-to (informative), explanation (scientific).
Newman's exposition. Britton's transactional function.

---

## Subtypes

### tutorial — learn by doing
The reader acquires a capability through guided practice.
Sequential, hands-on — the reader does something.

**Structure:**
- Clear learning outcome stated upfront
- Steps in logical sequence
- Include real friction points — "I got stuck here because..."
- "What I wish I knew earlier" moments are valuable
- Code examples where the code IS the learning
- Ends when the reader can do the thing

**Diátaxis:** Tutorial — acquisition mode, reader is studying not working

---

### how-to — task completion
The reader applies existing capability to accomplish a specific goal.
Practical, step-oriented — assumes some prior knowledge.

**Structure:**
- Goal stated clearly upfront
- Steps toward the goal
- Include warnings and edge cases
- "Why this step, not that" where non-obvious
- Ends when the task is complete

**Diátaxis:** How-to — application mode, reader is working not studying

---

### explanation — understand why
Builds the reader's mental model of a concept, system, or decision.
Not about doing — about understanding.

**Structure:**
- Problem or concept stated
- Why it works the way it does
- Analogies from unexpected domains where helpful
- Show evolution of understanding ("I used to think X...")
- Compare to related concepts
- Ends when the mental model is complete

**Diátaxis:** Explanation — understanding, not action

**Showcase variant:** Feature demonstrations, New and Noteworthy posts,
capability showcases are Article/explanation. If tied to a release,
cross-post with News/release as secondary.

---

### commentary — informed take
An informed take on a topic. Personal voice, expertise-led.
No formal thesis or structured conclusion. The author has a view and shares it.

**Structure:**
- Often triggered by external content (article, event, release)
- Quote or reference the trigger, then add one layer of analysis
- State the position directly — no hedging
- Shorter than an essay
- Often ends with an open question or ironic twist

**Catch-all rule:** If you intend a wider audience but it doesn't fit
tutorial/how-to/explanation/essay → use commentary.

---

### essay — argued to a conclusion
Has a clear position. Earns its hypothesis, counter-arguments, and conclusion
because it's trying to move the reader. See `forms/essay.md` for full guidance.

**In brief:** Opens with a claim. Builds evidence. Addresses counter-arguments
inline. Argues to a conclusion. Does not hedge the conclusion.

---

## What all Articles share

- Crafted for a wider audience — a cold reader can follow without prior context
- Provides background, context, or structure the reader needs
- Decoder-dominant — shaped for the reader's needs, not just the author's
- Standalone: each article works without requiring the reader to have been there

## Multi-part articles

Multi-part applies when scope requires splitting. Each part is standalone.
Essays more than explanations tend to be multi-part.
Series navigation added at publish time — not at write time.

## Length

No fixed target. Ends when the treatment is complete.
Explanation and essay tend to be longer (400–2000w).
Commentary tends to be shorter (200–600w).
Tutorial/how-to vary with the task.

## Opening and closing

**Opening:** Start with the claim, problem, or observation. Never announce
what will follow ("In this article I will explain...").

**Closing:** End when the point is made. A forward pointer is acceptable.
No summary. No "thanks for reading."
